What is Psychology?

Psychology is the study of people, including how they think, act, react, and interact. It is concerned with every part of behavior as well as the thoughts, feelings, and motivations that underpin them.

What is Psychologist?

Their job is to help their clients — those suffering from mental illnesses — comprehend how they feel, think, and behave.

In general, a psychologist can do diagnoses—the process of determining which specific mental health issue their client is experiencing.

They employ psychometric tests, clinical interviews and observations, therapeutic approaches, questionnaires, and exercises as diagnostic instruments. And, using the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders (DSM) criteria, psychologists can examine the outcomes of those investigations to help decide what condition their client has. After diagnosing the clients, psychologists can create treatment plans that will help them recover from the issues at hand.

What is Psychiatry?

Psychiatry is the study of mental health problems, including their diagnosis, treatment, and prevention. Psychiatrists are medical professionals who have completed psychiatric training. They frequently mix a broad general caseload with a unique area of expertise and study.

What is Psychiatrist?

Unlike psychologists and psychotherapists, psychiatrists often do not provide talk therapies, which are interventions that include a lot of talking. Rather, psychiatrists focus on addressing the chemical imbalances in the client's brain, which necessitates the use of drugs. Before pursuing professional training in psychiatry, a person must be a qualified medical doctor. Simply described, the typical process to becoming a psychiatrist would involve attending medical school and then completing psychiatric specialized study. Their extensive experience in this sector also qualifies doctors to prescribe and handle drugs.

Because they are doctors, they address mental health disorders in a more medical and physical manner, whereas psychologists and psychotherapists focus on non-medical therapies. With their extensive knowledge of medications, psychiatrists frequently collaborate with psychologists, particularly when treating clients with more severe mental health concerns. Although psychiatrists are less likely to provide talk treatments, they can diagnose clients using the DSM and, if necessary, recommend them to either a psychologist or a psychotherapist, depending on which one is best suited to their requirements.

What is Psychotherapy?

Individuals, groups, couples, and families all receive psychotherapy. Psychotherapists assist people in overcoming stress, emotional and interpersonal issues, and bad habits. There are many different approaches in psychotherapy:

  • cognitive behavioural therapies
  • psychoanalytic therapies
  • psychodynamic therapies
  • systematic and family psychotherapy
  • arts and play therapies
  • humanistic and integrative Psychotherapies
What is Psychotherapist?

A psychotherapist may be a psychiatrist, psychologist or other mental health professional, who has had further specialist training in psychotherapy.

Their treatment focuses on establishing a connection with their customers and investigating how their prior experiences may have contributed to their current problems. Psychotherapists can assist clients in discovering the underlying causes of their thoughts, feelings, and actions. Unlike psychiatrists, they teach their customers non-medical techniques to deal with mental health problems. Psychotherapists employ a variety of resources, depending on the modality they are trained in. They can aid in the rehabilitation process by providing the necessary tools.

Psychologist, Psychiatrist, and Psychotherapist: Which One is Right for You?
In most circumstances, people cannot contact a psychiatrist directly because a reference from another mental health expert - such as a psychologist, psychotherapist, or even a counselor or GP - is required.

However, if you or someone you know wants to (or is intending to) seek help, here are a few things to think about:

  • Severity Levels: Psychiatrists often work with clients who have more serious mental health problems. These are typically issues that are unlikely to be resolved just through conversation therapy. If you or a loved one is badly afflicted by such concerns, consulting a psychiatrist may be a better option. However, if the problem is small or moderate, a psychologist or a psychotherapist may be more suited, as talk therapies may be sufficient.

  • Do Your Research: Before contacting any mental health providers, think again. And, if you or someone you know is considering seeking treatment from a mental health expert, remember to choose wisely - make sure they are a qualified professional as well as someone you feel comfortable with. People should expect to be prescribed medicine when they visit a psychiatrist because they are doctors.

  • Your Commitment: Stay committed to the process regardless of which of the three specialists you desire to find or engage with. These treatments will most certainly take your time, money, and effort, depending on the severity of your mental health concerns. It is preferable to stick with treatment for the greatest results. And your efforts will almost probably pay off as your mental health improves.

If you are thinking about looking for help, but you're afraid, that you may don't have enough money, try looking for special programs with help in your area (e.g. mental health foundations, some even offer online counseling).

Early warning symptoms of mental illness differ depending on the conditions, however, there are several common indicators that may indicate the need for additional evaluation and support.

It's important to realize that experiencing one or a few of these symptoms does not necessarily indicate that you have a mental illness; nevertheless, if you or someone you know is experiencing multiple of these symptoms on a regular basis, obtaining professional help is advised.

Here are a few early warning signs to keep an eye out for:

  • Changes in Mood: Frequent mood swings, unexplained anger, sadness, or irritability that seem disproportionate to the situation.
  • Withdrawal: A tendency to isolate oneself and withdraw from friends, family, and social activities that were once enjoyed.
  • Difficulty Concentrating: Trouble focusing, making decisions, or remembering things that were previously manageable.
  • Changes in Sleep Patterns: Insomnia or sleeping too much, especially when it disrupts daily functioning.
  • Appetite Changes: Significant changes in appetite, either overeating or loss of appetite.
  • Loss of Interest: Losing interest or pleasure in activities that used to be enjoyable.
  • Fatigue or Low Energy: Feeling constantly tired or lacking energy, even without engaging in physically demanding activities.
  • Feeling of Hopelessness: Persistent feelings of hopelessness or worthlessness.
  • Unexplained Aches and Pains: Physical complaints without any apparent medical cause.
  • Increased Substance Use: Turning to alcohol, drugs, or other substances to cope with emotions or stress.
  • Unusual Thoughts: Having strange or unrealistic beliefs, or experiencing perceptual disturbances (hallucinations).
  • Troubled Thinking: Difficulty organizing thoughts, speaking coherently, or connecting ideas logically.
  • Heightened Sensitivity: Becoming unusually sensitive to sights, sounds, smells, or touch.
  • Increased Agitation: Restlessness, pacing, or heightened agitation.
  • Neglecting Responsibilities: Neglecting personal hygiene, work, or other responsibilities.
  • Lack of Emotion: Feeling emotionally numb or disconnected.
  • Uncharacteristic Risk-Taking: Engaging in risky behaviors without considering consequences.
It's essential to remember that mental health issues are complex and can manifest differently in different individuals. If you or someone you know is experiencing these signs and they are causing distress or impairment in daily life, it's important to reach out to a mental health professional for a proper assessment and appropriate support. Early intervention can make a significant difference in managing and treating mental health conditions effectively.

When early signs of mental illness are ignored or left untreated, the condition may progress, leading to more developed or severe symptoms. The specific signs of more developed mental illness can vary depending on the type of mental health condition, but some common indicators may include:

  • Intense and Persistent Symptoms: Symptoms that were initially mild or occasional become more intense, frequent, and persistent.
  • Severe Mood Swings: Mood swings become more severe and disruptive to daily life, with heightened periods of depression, anxiety, or mania.
  • Social Impairment: Social withdrawal increases, leading to significant difficulties in maintaining relationships and participating in social activities.
  • Deteriorating Work or Academic Performance: A decline in work or academic performance, difficulty concentrating, and struggling to complete tasks.
  • Increased Emotional Instability: Emotional responses become unpredictable and intense, making it challenging to regulate emotions.
  • Isolation and Alienation: The individual may feel isolated, misunderstood, or alienated from others due to the progression of their symptoms.
  • Self-Harm or Suicidal Ideation: A person may engage in self-harming behaviors or have persistent thoughts of suicide.
  • Disordered Thinking: More severe disorganization in thoughts, speech, or cognitive processes.
  • Loss of Touch with Reality: For conditions like schizophrenia, delusions, hallucinations, or disorganized thinking become more pronounced, leading to a disconnect from reality.
  • Impaired Functioning: Daily functioning is significantly impacted, leading to difficulties in maintaining employment, managing personal finances, or performing daily activities independently.
  • Physical Health Issues: Neglect of physical health and self-care may lead to worsening physical health conditions.
  • Substance Abuse: Increased reliance on drugs or alcohol as a means of coping with symptoms.
  • Aggressive or Hostile Behavior: Agitation, aggression, or hostility towards others may become more frequent.
  • Legal and Financial Problems: The individual may face legal issues or financial difficulties due to impaired judgment or reckless behavior.
It's essential to emphasize that mental health conditions can be effectively managed, even if the early signs were ignored. Seeking professional help at any stage is crucial. Even in more developed cases, there are treatment options available that can significantly improve a person's quality of life. If you or someone you know is experiencing any of these signs, it's important to reach out to a mental health professional for assessment and appropriate care. Early intervention and treatment can lead to better outcomes and increased well-being.

How can I support myself in a mental health crisis?

How can I help someone in a mental health crisis?

Supporting a person in a mental health crisis requires sensitivity, empathy, and understanding. Remember that it can be emotionally taxing. Make sure you also take care of your well-being and seek support from others if needed. Encouraging the person to seek professional help is crucial, as mental health professionals are trained to provide appropriate care and interventions during crises.

Here are some essential ways to provide support:

  • Stay Calm and Non-Judgmental: Remain calm and composed when interacting with the person. Avoid being judgmental or dismissive of their feelings and experiences.
  • Listen Actively: Listen attentively without interrupting or trying to offer immediate solutions. Let the person express their thoughts and emotions at their own pace.
  • Validate Their Feelings: Let the person know that their feelings are valid and understandable. Show empathy and understanding, even if you can't fully relate to their experiences.
  • Encourage Communication: Encourage the person to talk about what they are going through, but respect their boundaries if they're not ready to share.
  • Offer Reassurance: Provide reassurance that they are not alone and that you are there to support them through their difficult time.
  • Avoid Minimizing or Dismissing: Refrain from telling the person to "snap out of it" or that "things will get better" without understanding the depth of their struggles.
  • Ensure Safety: If the person expresses thoughts of self-harm or suicide, take it seriously. Stay with them if possible, and encourage them to reach out to a crisis helpline or mental health professional.
  • Avoid Making Promises: While offering support, avoid making promises that you cannot guarantee or fulfill.
  • Respect Autonomy: Respect the person's autonomy and involve them in decisions about their care as much as possible.
  • Encourage Professional Help: Encourage the person to seek professional help from a mental health counselor, therapist, or psychiatrist. Offer to assist them in finding resources or making appointments.
  • Be patient: Recovery takes time, and the person may experience ups and downs along the way. Be patient and continue offering your support.
  • Avoid Pressure: Refrain from pressuring the person to share details they are not comfortable discussing. Respect their boundaries.
  • Help with Practical Needs: Offer practical support, such as assisting with meals, household tasks, or transportation to appointments if needed.
  • Educate Yourself: Learn more about the person's specific mental health condition to better understand what they may be going through.
  • Seek Help from Others: If the situation is beyond your capacity to handle, involve other friends, family members, or mental health professionals to provide additional support.

There are also certain things you should avoid to ensure that your assistance is helpful and not harmful. Here are some examples:

  • Avoid Judgments and Stigmatizing Language: Refrain from using judgmental or stigmatizing language about mental health. Avoid terms like "crazy" or "insane" and understand that mental health issues are genuine medical conditions.
  • Don't Dismiss or Minimize Their Feelings: Avoid telling the person to "snap out of it" or "just get over it." Minimizing their feelings can make them feel invalidated and unheard.
  • Don't Try to Provide Simplistic Solutions: Avoid offering simple solutions to complex mental health issues. It's not as easy as just "being positive" or "thinking happy thoughts."
  • Don't Try to Handle Everything Yourself: While support is essential, remember that you are not a trained mental health professional. Encourage the person to seek professional help when needed.
  • Avoid Giving Ultimatums or Demands: Forcing someone to seek help or make changes can be counterproductive. Instead, encourage and support their willingness to seek help.
  • Don't Assume You Know Their Experience: Each person's mental health journey is unique. Avoid assuming you know what they're going through, and let them share their experiences in their own words.
  • Avoid Pressuring Them to Share: Respect the person's boundaries and avoid pressuring them to share details or experiences they are not comfortable discussing.
  • Don't Share Their Story Without Permission: Respect the person's privacy and avoid discussing their mental health struggles with others unless they have given explicit consent.
  • Don't Make it About You: While it's essential to offer your support, avoid turning the conversation around to be about your experiences or taking attention away from the person in crisis.
  • Don't Offer Unrequested Advice: Unless the person specifically asks for advice, avoid giving unsolicited advice, as it may come across as intrusive.
  • Avoid Enabling Harmful Behavior: While being empathetic, avoid enabling harmful behaviors, such as substance abuse or self-destructive actions.
  • Don't Dismiss Their Emotions: Never tell someone they have no reason to feel the way they do. Everyone's emotions and experiences are valid.
  • Avoid Overpromising: Be careful not to overpromise or make commitments you can't fulfill. Be realistic about the support you can provide.
  • Don't Ignore Red Flags: If the person is in immediate danger or exhibiting concerning behaviors, don't ignore the signs. Take action to ensure their safety, even if it means involving emergency services.
